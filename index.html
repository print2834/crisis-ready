Hurricane Preparedness Checklist 2025

Last Updated: May 5, 2025

Hurricane season requires serious preparation. High winds, heavy rain, storm surges, and power outages pose significant threats. This guide provides a comprehensive checklist to help you prepare your home and family well before a storm approaches. Remember, early preparation is key to safety.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Before Hurricane Season Starts:

  • Develop a Family Emergency Plan: Designate meeting points, communication methods, and evacuation routes. Practice the plan.
  • Build or Restock Your Emergency Kit: Include water (1 gallon per person/day for 3-7 days), non-perishable food, flashlight, batteries, first-aid supplies, medications, sanitation items, and important documents.
  • Check Insurance Coverage: Review your homeowner's or renter's insurance policy. Understand coverage for wind and flood damage (flood insurance is often separate).
  • Strengthen Your Home: Trim trees and shrubs around your property. Secure or bring in outdoor furniture, decorations, and trash cans. Consider installing storm shutters or boarding up windows.
  • Know Your Zone: Determine if you live in an evacuation zone and learn the designated routes.

When a Hurricane Watch is Issued:

  • Monitor Weather Updates: Stay informed through NOAA Weather Radio or local news outlets.
  • Fuel Vehicles and Generators: Gas stations may lose power or run out of fuel.
  • Gather Emergency Supplies: Ensure your kit is easily accessible.
  • Secure Your Home: Board up windows, secure doors, and bring in any remaining outdoor items.
  • Charge Devices: Keep cell phones, power banks, and radios fully charged.

When a Hurricane Warning is Issued:

  • Follow Evacuation Orders: If told to evacuate, do so immediately. Turn off gas, water, and electricity if instructed.
  • If Staying Home: Shelter in a small, interior, windowless room on the lowest level not prone to flooding. Stay away from windows and glass doors.
  • Listen Continuously: Keep your radio tuned for updates.

Wildfire Safety & Preparedness

Protect your home and family from the growing threat of wildfires.

Wildfires are becoming increasingly common and destructive in many parts of the US. Preparing your property ("hardening" your home) and having a solid evacuation plan are crucial for safety. This guide outlines key steps for wildfire preparedness.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Creating Defensible Space:

  • Zone 1 (0-5 feet): Remove all flammable vegetation, mulch, and items from under/around decks and structures. Use non-flammable landscaping like rocks or gravel.
  • Zone 2 (5-30 feet): Keep grass mowed short. Trim trees to create space between crowns and remove lower branches. Remove dead vegetation.
  • Zone 3 (30-100+ feet): Thin out dense vegetation and remove dead trees/shrubs.

Hardening Your Home:

  • Roof: Use Class A fire-rated roofing materials (asphalt shingles, metal, tile). Keep roofs and gutters clear of debris.
  • Vents: Cover attic, foundation, and eaves vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh to block embers.
  • Windows: Install dual-paned windows with tempered glass.
  • Siding & Decks: Use fire-resistant siding materials. Treat wooden decks with fire retardant or choose non-combustible materials.

Evacuation Planning:

  • Plan Multiple Routes: Know several ways out of your neighborhood and town.
  • Prepare a "Go Bag": Include essentials like medications, documents, water, snacks, masks (N95), goggles, and a change of clothes.
  • Stay Informed: Monitor local alerts (radio, apps, emergency services).
  • Leave Early: Evacuate as soon as orders are given, or even before if you feel unsafe. Wildfires move quickly.

Flood Readiness: Protect Your Property

Flooding can happen anywhere. Be prepared.

Floods are among the most common and costly natural disasters. Whether from heavy rain, river overflow, or storm surge, floodwaters can cause significant damage. Understanding your risk and taking preventative measures is essential.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Know Your Risk & Insurance:

  • Check Flood Maps: Visit FEMA's Flood Map Service Center to understand your area's flood risk.
  • Get Flood Insurance: Standard homeowner's insurance does NOT cover flood damage. Purchase flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or a private insurer. There's typically a 30-day waiting period.

Protect Your Property:

  • Elevate Utilities: Raise your furnace, water heater, and electrical panel if they are in flood-prone areas (basement).
  • Install Sump Pump: Ensure your sump pump is working and consider a battery backup.
  • Seal Foundation Cracks: Prevent water seepage.
  • Install Flood Vents: Allow floodwater to flow through foundation walls, reducing pressure.
  • Keep Drains Clear: Ensure gutters and storm drains are free of debris.

During a Flood Watch/Warning:

  • Move Valuables: Relocate important documents, electronics, and sentimental items to higher floors.
  • Secure Outdoor Items: Bring in or tie down anything that could float away.
  • Monitor Alerts: Stay tuned to local authorities and weather updates.
  • Evacuate if Ordered: Never drive or walk through floodwaters. "Turn Around, Don't Drown!"
  • Prepare Your Go-Bag: Have your emergency kit ready.

Power Outage Preparedness Plan

Stay safe and comfortable when the lights go out.

Power outages can happen anytime due to storms, equipment failure, or grid issues. Being prepared can make a significant difference in your comfort and safety, especially during extended outages.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Essential Supplies:

  • Lighting: Flashlights (LED preferred), headlamps, lanterns (battery-powered or solar). Have extra batteries. Avoid candles due to fire risk.
  • Communication: Battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA weather radio. Portable phone charger/power bank.
  • Food & Water: Non-perishable food items that don't require cooking. Manual can opener. Stored water.
  • Temperature Control: Blankets, sleeping bags for warmth. Battery-powered fans for cooling (if applicable).
  • First Aid Kit: Ensure it's stocked and accessible.
  • Medications: Have backups of essential medications, especially those requiring refrigeration (plan with your doctor/pharmacy).

During the Outage:

  • Report the Outage: Notify your utility company.
  • Food Safety: Keep refrigerator and freezer doors closed as much as possible. A full freezer stays cold longer. Use perishable food first.
  • Disconnect Appliances: Unplug sensitive electronics to prevent damage from power surges when electricity returns.
  • Generator Safety: If using a generator, operate it OUTDOORS and away from windows/doors to prevent carbon monoxide poisoning. Never run it in a garage or indoors.
  • Check on Neighbors: Especially the elderly or those with medical needs.
  • Stay Informed: Listen to your radio for updates.

Alternative Power Sources (Considerations):

  • Portable Generators: Good for powering essential appliances. Requires fuel and safe operation.
  • Standby Generators: Automatically turn on during an outage. More expensive installation.
  • Portable Power Stations (Solar Generators): Battery-based, can be recharged via solar panels or wall outlet. Quiet and fume-free. Good for electronics and small appliances.
  • Solar Chargers: For keeping phones and small devices charged.

Top Emergency Radios for 2025

Stay informed when other communication lines are down.

An emergency radio is a critical piece of preparedness gear. It allows you to receive vital weather alerts and news updates from NOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration) and local stations, even when power and internet are out. Look for radios with multiple power options (battery, hand-crank, solar).

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Key Features to Look For:

  • NOAA Weather Band with SAME Alerts: Automatically alerts you to warnings in your specific area.
  • Multiple Power Sources: Hand-crank, solar panel, rechargeable battery, and standard battery options provide reliability.
  • AM/FM Reception: Access to local news and information.
  • USB Charging Port: Ability to charge a smartphone or other small devices.
  • Flashlight: Built-in light source is a common and useful feature.
  • Durability & Water Resistance: Important for use in harsh conditions.

Essential Flashlights & Lighting

Reliable light sources are non-negotiable in an emergency.

When the power goes out, reliable lighting is crucial for safety and navigation. Modern LED technology offers bright, efficient, and long-lasting options. Consider having multiple types of lights for different situations.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Types of Emergency Lighting:

  • Handheld Flashlights (LED): Essential for everyone. Look for durable construction, water resistance, multiple brightness modes, and common battery types (AA or AAA are easy to find).
  • Headlamps: Keep your hands free for tasks. Ideal for navigating or working in the dark.
  • Lanterns (Battery/Solar): Provide area lighting for rooms or campsites. Look for long battery life and stable bases.
  • Glow Sticks: Safe, long-lasting, and waterproof light source, great for kids or marking locations.
  • Solar Garden Lights: Can be brought indoors during an outage for passive, low-level lighting.

Key Considerations:

  • Battery Type & Availability: Standardize if possible (e.g., all AA). Keep plenty of fresh batteries on hand, stored separately. Consider rechargeable batteries (like Eneloops) and a charger (solar or power bank compatible).
  • Brightness (Lumens): Higher lumens mean brighter light, but also drain batteries faster. Multiple modes are useful.
  • Durability & Water Resistance: Important for rough conditions. Look for IP ratings (e.g., IPX4 for water resistance).
  • Backup Options: Don't rely on just one light source.

Building Your Emergency First Aid Kit

Be ready to handle injuries when professional help might be delayed.

A well-stocked first aid kit is essential for any home, car, and emergency bag. In a disaster scenario, access to medical professionals could be limited, making basic first aid knowledge and supplies critical. You can buy pre-made kits or build your own.

Ad Placeholder (e.g., 300x250 or Responsive)

Essential First Aid Kit Contents:

  • Bandages (various sizes, adhesive strips, gauze pads, roller gauze)
  • Adhesive Tape
  • Antiseptic Wipes & Solution (e.g., hydrogen peroxide, iodine)
  • Antibiotic Ointment
  • Pain Relievers (Ibuprofen, Acetaminophen)
  • Antihistamines (for allergic reactions)
  • Hydrocortisone Cream (for itching)
  • Tweezers & Scissors
  • Disposable Gloves (Nitrile preferred)
  • CPR Breathing Barrier/Mask
  • Instant Cold Packs
  • Elastic Bandage (for sprains)
  • Triangular Bandage (for slings)
  • Safety Pins
  • Eye Wash Solution
  • Burn Gel or Dressing
  • Thermometer
  • First Aid Manual/Guide
  • Personal Medications (prescription & over-the-counter)
  • Emergency Blanket

Customization & Maintenance:

  • Consider Your Needs: Add items specific to your family (e.g., baby supplies, EpiPen).
  • Trauma Supplies (Optional but Recommended): Consider adding items like tourniquets, chest seals, and hemostatic gauze (requires proper training).
  • Check Expiration Dates: Regularly review your kit and replace expired items.
  • Location: Keep kits in accessible locations at home, in your car, and in your bug-out bag.
  • Get Training: Basic first aid and CPR training significantly increases your ability to help effectively.
